Shadows in the Feed

A story about screenshots, public shame, identity, activism and responsibility.

All stories ReWorld home

1. The Screenshot

The rumor began as a screenshot.

Someone had cut three messages out of a group chat, removed what came before and after, and posted them on an anonymous account. The image showed Ilyas's name, half of his profile picture, and the words: she only does it for attention.

By lunch, half the school had seen it.

Mina saw it on the bus. First her hands went cold. Then her face became hot. The post was about her. On Saturday she had spoken in the town square about climate, Gaza, loneliness, and all the things adults called complicated even when they hit young people directly in the body.

She had not claimed to be an expert. She had said, "I cannot pretend this does not concern us."

Now she was on a story with a crying emoji and the caption: When activism becomes content.

Ilyas was standing by the lockers when she arrived.

"Did you write it?" she asked.

He looked down. "Not like that."

"Your name is there."

"It is out of context."

She laughed once, not because anything was funny. "The sentence exists."

People around them became quiet in the way people become quiet when they pretend not to listen.

Sara came over. She wore a small cross around her neck and had no patience for unfairness. "Say sorry," she told Ilyas.

"You do not know what happened," he said.

"I know she is being exposed."

Jonas stood behind Sara. He was suspicious of anything that smelled like group pressure. "And now we expose him back?"

"It is called accountability," Sara said.

"It can also be revenge with better language," Jonas said.

Mina wanted to shout at everyone to shut up. Instead she went to the bathroom, locked the door and opened her phone.

The feed was waiting.

It had already decided who she was.

2. The Council Is Opened

That evening, Mina, Ilyas, Sara and Jonas sat in the library. Not because they were friends. Because the class teacher had said that if this continued, parents and school leadership would be brought in.

On the table stood an old screen. The words on it were: ReWorld Council: conversation opened.

The Cosmos Minister came first, not as a person, but as a calm voice in the room.

"This is not a trial," the voice said. "But it is not a game either. Someone has been harmed. Someone may be harmed more. We will examine without turning people into weapons."

"He wrote it," Mina said.

"She made the speech about herself," Ilyas said.

"There," Jonas said. "There it is again."

"Stop," said The Cosmos Minister. "First rule: nobody may use pain as smoke. Second rule: nobody may use truth as a knife without responsibility."

The Christian voice asked how they were treating the person with the least protection right now. Today that seemed to be Mina. Tomorrow it could be Ilyas. Love of neighbor did not mean pretending nothing had happened. It meant refusing to enjoy a fall.

The Muslim voice said that information must be examined before it damages people. Justice required more than feeling certain. It required discipline, especially when the group was pushing.

The Buddhist voice asked what suffering each of them was trying to escape by attacking.

No one answered.

Plato's voice said, "You are looking at shadows on a wall. The screenshot is a shadow. The comment section is an echo. The question is who is carrying the figures in front of the light."

The power-critical voice asked who gained from the fight. The anonymous account gained followers. The audience gained drama. The school gained a problem it could call student conflict instead of asking why rumors had so much power.

Mina felt her breathing speed up.

"So it is my fault too?" she asked.

"No," said The Cosmos Minister. "Harm should not be spread equally just to look balanced. Whoever posted the image without context did something wrong. Whoever spreads it takes part. But we still have to examine what actually happened."

3. Who Gets to Be a Person?

Ilyas told the story at last.

He had seen Mina's speech. He had been irritated because she mentioned religion and war in the same paragraph. Not cruelly, but quickly. It gave him the old feeling: people saw him as Muslim before they saw him as Ilyas.

"I wrote to Amir that I was tired of people using other people's suffering to build identity," he said. "Then I wrote that sentence. I did not mean Mina does not care. I meant social media makes everything into attention."

"But you wrote she," Mina said.

"Yes."

"And it hit me."

"Yes," he said quietly.

Kant's voice entered the room. A human being must not be used only as a tool. Not Mina as a symbol of fake activism. Not Ilyas as a symbol of religious sensitivity. Not Sara as a symbol of moral policing. Not Jonas as a symbol of cynicism. When they turned each other into examples, they lost the person.

The scientific voice sorted what was known. The screenshot was real but incomplete. The publication increased harm. No one knew who posted it. No one knew what the school would believe tomorrow. Therefore actions had to be based on what could be investigated, not on panic.

The psychological voice explained shame. Shame makes people fast. It says: attack, flee, freeze or collapse. Group identity makes it faster. When my people are threatened, I may start defending everything they do. When the other side makes a mistake, I may believe the mistake shows who they really are.

Sara looked at the table. "I only wanted to protect Mina."

"Protection is necessary," said the peacebuilder. "But protection must separate boundary from punishment. A boundary says: this must stop. Revenge says: now you must feel what I felt."

The child's voice asked, "Why do people have to be publicly ashamed before you believe something is serious?"

The room became completely still.

4. A Post That Does Not Perform Innocence

The next day there was a new post from the anonymous account. It showed another fragment, this time from Mina's speech. The caption said: Main character politics.

Now the room changed. People who had defended Mina became unsure. People who disliked Ilyas became excited. The feed did what the feed does best. It made everyone feel late to the truth.

The council met again.

The Cosmos Minister asked for two lists: What must stop? What must be repaired?

What must stop: anonymous exposure, screenshots without context, turning identity into a weapon, demanding instant confession for public entertainment, and using complexity to avoid responsibility.

What must be repaired: Mina's safety at school, Ilyas's right not to be reduced to one sentence, the class's habit of forwarding before thinking, and the school's silence around public shame.

Mina and Ilyas wrote a joint statement, but it was not a performance of friendship.

They wrote that the screenshot was real and incomplete. They wrote that posting private messages for attention was harmful. Ilyas wrote that his sentence was careless and unfair to Mina. Mina wrote that activism must leave room for the people it speaks about, not only the speaker's own moral feeling.

They did not write that everything was fine.

Sara helped draft a plan for a meeting about digital responsibility. Jonas asked that the meeting include the danger of moral crowds, not only bullying. The teacher agreed, but only after Mina said, "If adults only tell us to be kind online, they have not understood the machine."

No one found the anonymous account that week.

That mattered. The story did not close neatly.

But something had shifted. More students stopped sharing posts without asking what had been cut away. A few admitted they liked the drama. One person said, "I think I feel important when I know something ugly before others do."

That sentence was not pretty. It was useful.

5. After the Feed Moves On

A month later, the school was talking about something else.

The feed had moved on. Feeds always move on. Bodies do not move on as quickly.

Mina still checked rooms before entering them. Ilyas still felt watched when religion came up in class. Sara still wanted clear lines between right and wrong. Jonas still distrusted crowds. None of that vanished.

But they had a sentence now: do not turn people into examples before you have seen the person.

The Cosmos Minister left them with a final question.

If everyone can be reduced to one screenshot, what kind of world are we training ourselves to live in?

No one answered in the comments.

For once, that was a beginning.
