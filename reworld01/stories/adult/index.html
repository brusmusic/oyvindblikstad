Adult Rooms, Children's Bill

A story about family, work, politics, war, media and what adults pass on.

All stories ReWorld home

1. The Table

The Cosmos Minister opened the meeting without ceremony.

"Today we will not ask why children quarrel," she said. "We will ask why adults, with salaries, voting rights, passwords, budgets, laws, weapons, positions, sermons, comment sections and family traditions, still manage to send the bill downward."

No one laughed.

Around the table sat the ReWorld Council: Christian, Muslim, Buddhist, Platonic, Kantian, scientific, psychological, child-centered, power-critical and peacebuilding voices.

Outside the room, the world continued. Parents answered sharply while children waited for eye contact. Leaders called burnout a problem of resilience. Politicians sold fear as agency. Media companies packaged anger as engagement. Religious and secular people suspected each other of destroying society. Families carried old wounds as if they were heirlooms.

"The elephant in the room," said the power-critical voice, "is that many adults say they want to protect children, but first protect their own position."

The child's voice looked down at the table.

"Then why do you not just say that?"

2. The Family as the First Institution

The psychological voice began.

"The family is not only private. It is the child's first state, first economy, first justice system, first religion, even when the family is secular. It is also the first workplace, because the child learns what work means through adult absence, stress and attention."

The Christian voice said that love of neighbor must begin with the smallest person in the room. Not with the family image. Not with reputation. Not with a parent's need to appear good.

The Muslim voice said responsibility was not only a feeling. It was stewardship. The strong had responsibility toward the vulnerable. Community without justice became clan. Discipline without mercy became hardness.

The child asked, "If adults love me, why do I have to carry their mood?"

The silence after the question was exact.

Psychology answered carefully. "Because many adults have carried someone else's mood. Trauma travels not only as memory. It travels as tone, silence, control, overreaction and absence. But explanation is not acquittal."

The Cosmos Minister wrote: Wounds explain. Wounds do not give the right to wound.

The power-critical voice added that a family can become a small regime. The person who controls money, housing, inheritance, silence or shame can make others call fear loyalty.

Kant's voice said the child must never become a tool for an adult's self-image, marriage, status, revenge or loneliness.

The Buddhist voice said attachment can be confused with ownership. Desire can look like love. Fear can call itself care.

3. Work, Class and the Exhausted Home

The scientific voice opened the next file.

"Work is often measured in productivity, sick leave and growth. We also need to measure what it does to nervous systems, families and trust. A society that uses people up and then offers courses in coping has not understood the problem."

Plato's voice said many people saw only shadows: title, income, address, body, network, accent. They mistook the shadow for the human being.

"Class mobility is often told as a solo story," said the power-critical voice. "I worked hard, therefore I deserve my place. What disappears is who carried the shame, who paid with health, who was left behind, and which doors were never open to everyone."

The Christian voice said greed is not only a private vice. It can be organized as a bonus model.

The Muslim voice said justice requires more than charity afterward. The deal itself must be examined: the wage, the risk, the dignity, the distribution of time.

Kant asked whether we could will a rule where people must sell health, time with children and dignity in order to survive. If not, the system was not merely hard. It was unjust.

The peacebuilder did not soften the diagnosis. But she added that change must be built so people have a path back from denial. A leader who is only publicly shamed often defends the institution harder. An institution that must admit harm needs both pressure and a practical route to repair.

The child asked, "Why are you so tired when you come home?"

No one at the table answered quickly. It was an adult question wearing a child's clothes.

4. Meaning, Power and Fear

The Cosmos Minister wrote three words: meaning, power, fear.

"Religion can give meaning," she said. "Secularity can protect freedom. Both can be misused."

The Christian voice said faith that hides abuse, subordinates women, buys loyalty or decorates power is not holiness. It is hypocrisy.

The Muslim voice said reducing Islam to suspicion creates injustice, and using Islam for coercion, violence or honor control destroys the dignity of faith.

The Buddhist voice said peaceful language can become passivity. If calm is used to tolerate injustice without resistance, calm has become a mask.

The scientific voice said secular institutions are not neutral simply because they say they are neutral. They can carry class blindness, racism, technocratic arrogance and economic interest.

The power-critical voice did not smile. "All large languages can be captured: God, people, freedom, security, tradition, market, nation, research. Always ask: Who gets to define reality? Who gets called extreme? Who gets called normal?"

Plato's voice warned that if people see only shadows, they can vote for shadows, hate shadows and die for shadows.

Kant's voice said rights are not decoration for good days. They are limits on what majorities, states, families and markets may do to a person.

The child asked, "Can I believe differently from my mother and father without losing them?"

Every civilization should be able to answer yes.

5. War and the Cheap Enemy

The peacebuilder spoke next.

"Nonviolence is not pretending violence does not exist. It is refusing to make harm a method for pride. It is resistance without dehumanization. It is protection of civilians. It is truth without intoxication by revenge."

The power-critical voice answered, "But some people use dialogue as a sleeping medicine. They ask the harmed to wait, to be nuanced, to understand the abuser, while harm continues."

"Yes," said the peacebuilder. "That is why protection must come first when people are in danger. Dialogue without safety can decorate oppression."

The psychological voice said war dysregulates groups. Fear narrows sight. Grief becomes rage. Humiliation becomes identity. Children learn the enemy's name before they learn the enemy's face.

The Buddhist voice said hatred does not end through more hatred, but this must not be misused to ask victims to be silent. Stopping harm can be compassion.

The Christian voice said forgiveness without truth is cheap. Reconciliation without responsibility is a new injustice.

The Muslim voice said justice must apply even when we hate. Especially then. Hatred makes people willing to call transgression necessary.

The scientific voice had numbers no one wanted to read aloud. The cost of war for children was not only death and injury. It was broken school, broken sleep, lost language, distrust of adults, bodies that never rested.

The child's voice asked, "When adults say children are the future, why do they bomb the future?"

The Cosmos Minister did not write. She let the sentence stand alone.

6. The Shadow Factory

Plato's voice stood.

"Our caves have become portable," he said. "We carry them in our hands. Shadows arrive all day. A headline. A clip. A face. A flag. A confession. A humiliation. We react before we have seen."

The media economy was not one villain. It was a set of incentives. Outrage held attention. Attention became money. Fear became loyalty. Loyalty became power. Power defended the system that produced more outrage.

The scientific voice said this was measurable. The psychological voice said it was embodied. The power-critical voice said it was profitable. The child said it was frightening.

"So what do adults do?" Øyvind asked from the edge of the room.

The council did not answer with a slogan.

They answered with obligations.

Slow down the machine where you have control. Do not reward humiliation with attention. Build institutions where truth can arrive before scandal. Teach children how images can lie without being fake. Protect public conversation from becoming a market for nervous systems. Refuse the pleasure of having an enemy too cheap to understand.

7. The Bill

At the end, The Cosmos Minister returned to the first sentence.

"Adults send the bill downward when they avoid the cost of truth. They send it to children as anxiety. To young people as cynicism. To workers as exhaustion. To minorities as suspicion. To nature as extraction. To future generations as debt. To enemies as permission to become less human."

No one objected.

"The way forward," she continued, "is not innocence. Adults will not become pure. Institutions will not become kind because we use better words. But adults can become more accountable. Institutions can be designed to hear truth earlier. Power can be made more answerable. Families can stop calling fear loyalty. Schools can teach conflict before conflict becomes identity. Workplaces can measure human cost. Politics can stop selling panic as courage."

Øyvind asked the question quietly.

"Can adults be friends?"

The child answered before the council.

"Only if they stop making children pay for the parts of themselves they refuse to face."

The room did not become hopeful in a simple way.

It became clearer.

Perhaps that is where adult hope begins: not in comfort, but in the end of pretending.
