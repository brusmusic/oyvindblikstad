# Why Do We Need Each Other?

## Adult Book: The Shared Body

### 1. The Failure

The city learned dependence through failure.

Not collapse.

Something smaller.

A heatwave.

A water warning.

A hospital delay.

A power outage in two districts.

Public transport reduced.

Supply trucks late.

Phones still working, which made everyone feel informed and helpless at the same time.

At first, people asked who was to blame.

That question mattered.

But it did not bring water to the elderly on the sixth floor.

It did not cool the apartment with no shade.

It did not check insulin in a broken refrigerator.

It did not tell parents whether school would open.

The Cosmos Minister listened as the Council gathered.

The Child asked:

**Why do adults call dependence weakness until the systems stop working?**

### 2. Infrastructure

Science described the heat, the grid, the water system, the hospitals, the supply chain.

The Living World described soil, rivers, trees, air, and the cost of pretending the city was separate from them.

Psychology described panic, denial, fatigue, and the strange pride that keeps people from asking for help.

The Power Critic asked who had backup generators, private transport, flexible work, savings, and second homes.

Christianity asked who was left alone.

Islam asked what mutual obligation required beyond charity.

Buddhism asked what illusions of separateness were collapsing.

Kant asked whether people were being treated as ends or as acceptable losses.

The Peacebuilder asked what could be repaired before anger hardened into factions.

The Council did not make dependence romantic.

Dependence can be exploited.

Need can be used to control.

Care work can be made invisible.

But denial of dependence is also dangerous.

It leaves systems unprepared and people ashamed of being human.

### 3. The Invisible Map

The official map showed roads, pipes, hospitals, substations, schools, and shelters.

The real map included more.

Who checked on the widow in apartment 6B.

Who knew which teenager could translate for a grandmother.

Which shopkeeper allowed people to charge phones.

Which bus driver knew the informal stops.

Which nurse had already worked too many hours.

Which trees made a street survivable.

Which basement stayed cool.

Which people had been ignored so long that they no longer trusted instructions.

A city is not only infrastructure.

It is remembered relationship.

When relationship is weak, every emergency becomes more expensive.

When trust is broken, even good information arrives late.

### 4. The Elephants

There were elephants in this room too.

One was inequality.

The wealthy call resilience what the poor experience as abandonment.

One was ecological debt.

The city had removed trees, sealed soil, expanded heat, and called it development.

One was gendered labor.

Care appeared when needed and disappeared when counted.

One was individualism.

People had been taught to say independence when they meant isolation with better branding.

The Living World spoke plainly:

"You are not outside the living system. You are an organ pretending to be a whole body."

The sentence was uncomfortable.

It was also useful.

### 5. Mutual Aid and Public Duty

Neighbors began helping.

They shared fans, water, rides, food, information, and shade.

This mattered.

But The Power Critic raised a hand.

"Do not let mutual aid become an excuse for public failure."

The Council held the tension.

People need one another directly.

People also need institutions that do not abandon them to kindness.

Charity can save a life today.

Justice asks why that life depended on charity.

Care is warm.

Infrastructure is care made durable.

The adult answer to why we need each other cannot stop at feelings.

It must include budgets, maintenance, climate adaptation, labor rights, public health, housing, and trust.

Otherwise need becomes a poem used to hide neglect.

### 6. The Shared Body

After the crisis, the city wanted to move on.

It always does.

Moving on is easier than being changed.

But the Council left a different image:

The city as a shared body.

Water as circulation.

Air as breath.

Transport as legs.

Care as nervous system.

Public trust as immune response.

The Living World as the larger body in which the city lives.

No metaphor is perfect.

This one was useful because it made a lie harder:

That one part can suffer indefinitely while the whole remains healthy.

Blix asked why we need each other.

The Council did not say because humans are good.

Humans are mixed.

The Council said:

**We need each other because no life remains private at the level of consequence.**

The final question did not comfort the room.

It organized it.

**What would change if we designed society around honest dependence rather than the performance of independence?**

### Reflection Questions

1. Where do you see dependence hidden under the language of independence?
2. What invisible labor keeps your community functioning?
3. How can mutual aid help without excusing institutional failure?
4. What does ecological dependence reveal about human dignity?
5. What would care look like if it were designed into infrastructure?
