# Why Can't We Build It Together?

## Youth Book: The City in the Server Room

### 1. The Project

The assignment was to design a digital space for young people in the town.

Not an app exactly.

Not a game exactly.

A place.

"A youth city," Aksel said.

"That sounds like something adults say when they want funding," Samir replied.

Maja opened the design board.

Iben created folders.

Nora asked who the space was for.

"Young people," Aksel said.

"Which ones?"

"All of them."

Nora waited.

"All of them is not a design," she said.

That was how the argument began.

### 2. The Default User

Their first version looked good.

It had events, chat rooms, music, local news, volunteer options, and a map of safe places.

The colors were calm. The buttons worked. The presentation impressed the teacher.

Then the test group arrived.

One student could not read the small text.

One did not want real names visible.

One asked what happened if a bully joined the same room.

One asked why there were no quiet spaces.

One asked why everything required fast internet.

One said, "This feels like it was made for people who already join things."

That sentence did not break the project.

It broke the illusion that the project was finished.

### 3. The Council Enters

Mina placed the ReWorld Council cards beside the laptops.

Science asked for user evidence.

Psychology asked what made a digital room feel safe or unsafe.

The Power Critic asked who controlled visibility.

Kant asked whether users were being treated as people or data.

Plato asked whether the beautiful prototype was only a shadow of inclusion.

Islam asked about responsibility to the whole community.

Christianity asked who had been designed to the edge.

Buddhism asked what attachment to praise was preventing them from seeing.

The Child asked:

**Why do you call it for everyone before you have asked the people who are hardest to include?**

Aksel closed his laptop halfway.

"So we have to start over?"

The Cosmos Minister listened.

Mina read:

**Not everything must be destroyed when a hidden assumption is found. But the assumption must lose its right to steer in secret.**

### 4. Building With, Not For

They invited more testers.

Not as decoration.

As co-designers.

That changed the work.

It became slower.

It became less elegant.

It became more real.

The anonymous reporting feature created arguments about false reports.

The real-name profile option created arguments about safety and accountability.

The quiet room created arguments about whether silence was antisocial.

The local resource map created arguments about who could be trusted.

The Council did not remove disagreement.

It made disagreement harder to hide under clean design.

Samir wanted strong moderation.

Aksel worried that too many rules would kill the space.

Nora said both could be true.

"A city without rules becomes dangerous," she said. "A city with only rules becomes dead."

No one had a quick answer.

That was progress.

### 5. The Unbuilt Door

The final presentation did not show a perfect youth city.

It showed a prototype and a list of unresolved decisions.

At first Aksel hated that.

"It makes us look unfinished."

"We are unfinished," Iben said.

They presented the missing pieces as part of the work:

Who moderates?

Who appeals a moderation decision?

Who protects vulnerable users?

Who prevents adults from taking over?

Who notices the young people who never log in?

The town committee was quiet.

One adult said, "This is more complicated than we expected."

Maja answered before she could become afraid:

"Yes. That is why young people should not only be consulted after the decision."

### 6. Together

Afterward, they sat in the server room because it was the only place available.

The machines hummed.

The unfinished prototype glowed on the screen.

The Child's question remained:

**Who do we mean when we say we?**

In childhood, that question had lived in a hut.

Now it lived in permissions, profiles, moderation tools, internet access, and trust.

The shape had changed.

The question had not.

The Cosmos Minister did not congratulate them.

The Cosmos Minister asked:

**What must remain open so that the people not yet present can still enter?**

No one answered.

They saved the question as a design requirement.

### Reflection Questions

1. Who was the "default user" in the first version?
2. What is the difference between designing for people and designing with them?
3. Why can a beautiful system still exclude?
4. What should remain open in a project meant for a whole community?
5. Who is missing from the rooms where your world is designed?
