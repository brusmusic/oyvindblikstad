# Why Can't Everyone Just Be Friends?

## Youth Book: Shadows in the Feed

### 1. The Clip

The clip was seven seconds long.

That was enough.

It showed Samir pushing Aksel in the hallway. Aksel hit the lockers. Someone gasped. Someone laughed. Then the video stopped.

By lunch, half the school had seen it.

By the end of the day, people who had never spoken to Samir were calling him violent.

The caption said:

**So this is who he really is.**

Iben watched it three times.

Each time, the clip became more convincing.

Not because it showed more.

Because everyone around it sounded so certain.

Maja sent a message:

**Did you see?**

Iben replied:

**Yes.**

Then she typed:

**But what happened before?**

She deleted it.

It is easier to doubt in your head than in a group chat.

### 2. The Part Before

The next morning, Mina asked Samir, Aksel, Iben, Maja, and two student representatives to come to the library.

The ReWorld Council cards were already on the table.

"We are not here to decide who gets destroyed," Mina said. "We are here to examine what happened and what must stop."

"The video is clear," Aksel said.

"It is short," Mina replied. "Those are not the same."

Samir looked exhausted.

"I pushed him," he said. "I am not saying I didn't."

"Then why are we here?" Aksel asked.

Iben finally said the thing she had deleted.

"What happened before?"

There was another video.

Longer.

Messier.

It showed Aksel blocking Samir's way. It showed two boys laughing. It showed someone saying, "Say it in your language then." It showed Samir trying to leave.

Then it showed the push.

Nobody looked proud after watching it.

The Child card lay in the middle.

**Why do people love the part where someone breaks, but ignore the part where they were bent?**

### 3. The Council Speaks

Science asked what evidence existed.

Psychology asked what happens to a body under pressure.

The Power Critic asked who gained attention from posting the shortest clip.

The Peacebuilder asked what needed to stop immediately.

Kant asked whether a person should be reduced to their worst seven seconds.

Islam asked about responsibility without humiliation.

Christianity asked who was being cast outside the circle of human worth.

Buddhism asked what anger was protecting.

Plato asked whether the feed showed reality or shadows arranged to look like truth.

No voice erased the push.

No voice erased the humiliation before it.

The Council did not balance harm like weights on a scale.

It kept more than one truth in the room.

The Cosmos Minister listened.

Then Mina read:

**A person can be responsible for harm and still be more than the image of that harm. A group can be hurt and still be responsible for what it does with the hurt.**

### 4. The Crowd

The school wanted a simple story.

Some wanted Samir punished.

Some wanted Aksel exposed.

Some wanted the whole thing to disappear because it made everyone uncomfortable.

The feed wanted speed.

The Council required attention.

Those two things do not naturally like each other.

Maja admitted she had shared the clip.

"I didn't add anything," she said.

"Sharing is adding movement," Mina said.

That sentence sat down beside her.

Aksel admitted he had blocked Samir.

"I was joking."

Samir looked at him.

"You were not the one being joked into a corner."

Nobody corrected the sentence.

It needed to stand.

### 5. Repair Is Not Erasing

The first action was protection.

The clip came down from the school groups.

Not because the push did not matter.

Because public punishment by a crowd is not the same as justice.

Then came responsibility.

Samir apologized for pushing.

Not for being angry.

For using his body to frighten someone.

Aksel apologized for blocking him and for the words that made the hallway smaller.

Not perfectly.

But without jokes.

Maja posted a correction:

**I shared a short clip without context. That helped make a person smaller. I should not have done that.**

Some people called it dramatic.

Some people respected it.

Both were survivable.

### 6. The Unanswered Part

A week later, Iben saw the crack in the schoolyard again.

The younger children were playing near it.

Their lines were still visible.

The older students had better phones, faster jokes, sharper captions.

But the question was not new.

It had only changed clothes.

Blix had once asked the Council why everyone could not just be friends.

The youth version was less innocent:

**What has to be true in a community before friendship is more than a performance?**

The Cosmos Minister did not answer too quickly.

That was the answer's beginning.

### Reflection Questions

1. Why did the short clip feel so convincing?
2. What is the difference between context and excuse?
3. When does sharing become participation?
4. Can someone be responsible without being reduced to their worst moment?
5. What would a slower, more honest feed require from us?
