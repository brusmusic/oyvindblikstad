# Why Do We Need Each Other?

## Youth Book: Shared Air

### 1. The Notification

The notification came during math.

**School closed early due to air quality warning.**

At first, everyone cheered.

Then they looked out the window.

The sky was not dramatic.

It was pale and heavy.

The hills beyond town had disappeared behind smoke from forest fires far away.

"But the fires are not even here," Aksel said.

Nora looked at the air sensor on the classroom wall.

"The air is."

That was the first lesson.

Distance does not always protect you.

Sometimes the world arrives by breathing.

### 2. The Group Chat

By afternoon, the group chat was full.

Some students joked about apocalypse weather.

Some shared masks.

Some mocked the warning.

Some worried about grandparents, asthma, younger siblings, and outdoor workers.

One message said:

**Why is everyone overreacting?**

Another answered:

**Because some people have lungs that react before your opinion does.**

Maja sent nothing.

She was tired of chats where every sentence became a team.

Iben suggested a school response group.

Aksel said it sounded like a committee.

Samir said committees were what people invented when they wanted responsibility to move slowly.

Nora wrote:

**Maybe we need both speed and responsibility.**

No one liked that answer.

Which meant it was probably useful.

### 3. The Council in the Gym

The next day, the students gathered in the gym because the ventilation was better there.

Mina brought the Council.

Science explained particulate matter, wind patterns, lungs, and exposure.

Psychology asked why invisible danger creates either panic or denial.

The Living World reminded them that humans are not outside the systems they damage.

The Power Critic asked who had clean indoor air, paid sick leave, transport, and the ability to stay home.

Christianity asked who was most vulnerable.

Islam asked what responsibility looked like when one person's convenience became another person's risk.

Buddhism asked what fear made people mock what they did not want to feel.

Kant asked whether they could universalize ignoring warnings because they personally felt fine.

The Child raised a hand:

**Why do adults call it "the environment" as if it is somewhere else?**

Nobody answered quickly.

### 4. The List

They made a list of needs.

Masks for students with asthma.

A quiet room for those feeling anxious.

Check-ins for younger students walking home.

Messages in clear language, not panic language.

A plan for outdoor workers at the school.

A way to share air-quality data without turning it into social drama.

Then Samir added:

**Ask why this keeps happening.**

Aksel groaned.

"Can we solve one thing without solving the planet?"

The Cosmos Minister listened.

Mina read:

**Action does not become meaningless because it is incomplete. But incomplete action becomes dishonest if it forbids larger questions.**

So they kept both lists.

What helps today?

What must change over time?

### 5. Dependence

For three days, school felt different.

The strongest runners stayed inside.

The quiet students became useful because they noticed who was missing.

The students with asthma became teachers of reality.

The cleaners knew which rooms held dust.

The caretaker knew the ventilation system better than any official email.

The nurse knew which students pretended they were fine.

Need rearranged status.

Not perfectly.

But enough to show the old order was not the only possible one.

Maja said, "It is strange that the people we overlook are the ones who know how things actually work."

Nora replied, "Maybe that is why they are overlooked."

The Power Critic did not need to speak.

The sentence had done its work.

### 6. Larger Than Alone

When the air cleared, everyone wanted to return to normal.

That was understandable.

Normal is comfortable because it asks so little imagination.

But the Council had left a question in the gym:

**If we breathe shared air, what else do we share without admitting it?**

The answer was not sentimental.

They shared risk.

They shared infrastructure.

They shared habits.

They shared consequences.

They shared a future even when they did not share opinions.

Blix had once asked why we need each other.

The youth version sounded like this:

**What kind of freedom remembers dependence instead of pretending to escape it?**

The Cosmos Minister did not close the question.

The students carried it out into the clearer air.

### Reflection Questions

1. Why did some students mock the air-quality warning?
2. Who became more visible when breathing became difficult?
3. What is the difference between today's help and long-term change?
4. How can people share consequences without sharing beliefs?
5. What kind of freedom still remembers dependence?
